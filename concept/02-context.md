# Context — Trinidad & Tobago

## Trust crisis

In June 2026, Trinidad & Tobago mourned **12-year-old Mercedez Layne**, whose hire-car journey ended in tragedy. The trip was described as short and routine: the kind of movement thousands of children make daily. Public grief turned into **fear of cars for hire** and ad-hoc advice: photograph the vehicle, driver, and licence plate before boarding.

External coverage: [Jamaica Observer, 8 June 2026](https://www.jamaicaobserver.com/2026/06/08/suspect-custody-trinidad-body-missing-12-year-old-found/).

That advice helps **identification after harm**; it does not **prevent** harm, **detect deviation**, or **coordinate help** while a child is in transit.

## Driver-side harm

Separately, drivers report **robbery setups** — passengers requesting detours into areas where the driver is ambushed. In at least one reported case, a driver had to **accelerate into an attacker** when passengers appeared complicit. Good drivers bear the same reputational damage as bad actors.

## Geography — looking at T&T from above

Trinidad is not one urban blob. From overhead you see:

- **Urban corridors** — POS, east–west corridor, San Fernando, Chaguanas, Arima: more hire options, more people online, faster emergency response (still imperfect).
- **Semi-rural fringe** — longer legs between zones, mixed car ownership.
- **Rural and remote** — Cedros, Moruga, Tabaquite interior, northeast coast, dispersed Tobago communities: **hire car may be the only option**; trips are often **ad hoc between family homes**; **emergency response measured in tens of minutes**.

Child transport is **not school-only**. Common patterns:

- Unofficial **family home ↔ family home** handoffs
- **Lessons** — pan, dance, tutoring, sports outside school hours
- Church, clinic, sports practice

## Industry landscape

- Formal **hire-car permits** (Transport Board) coexist with informal street hire.
- Parents often use **word-of-mouth** drivers — no app, no receipt, no live state.
- Mobile data is **uneven** — urban 4G vs rural dropouts; SMS remains relevant.

## Cultural context

T&T has a large population of **chronically online** residents — often underemployed, politically vocal, sometimes described locally as *macco* (minding others' business). This project asks whether that energy can be **redirected** into structured witness work rather than unstructured social media justice.
